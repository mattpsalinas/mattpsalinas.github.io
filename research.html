Compton Blind Thrust Fault Paleoseismology

The Compton thrust is an active 40-km-long blind thrust fault extending beneath the western part of metropolitan Los Angeles. A previous study by my group developed a paleoseismic record for the northern segment of the Compton through characterization and dating of folded shallow sediments along the back-limb axial trace of the associated growth fault-bend fold. I am working on a similar dataset that was collected for the southern segment, analysis of which we would use to determine whether the Compton thrust ruptures in single- or multi-segment ruptures, or both. Additionally, this new data would be used to help quantify past strain release rates (and therefore) future earthquake hazard on the Compton thrust.

Geothermal Induced Seismicity -- Undergraduate Project

The classically favored mechanism for driving induced seismicity is pore pressure, in which earthquakes are induced through a lowering of effective normal stress. For this research, we explored other potential mechanisms that might influence induced seismicity in Enhanced Geothermal Systems (EGS). In comparing spatiotemporal variations in the b-value (specifically b-positive) of nine different EGS injection sequences, we found that sequences experience anomalously high b-values at the outermost edges of the sequence, which we attribute to the effects of aseismic slip. We think aseismic slip plays an important role in EGS induced seismicity that should be better characterized and accounted for in future EGS studies and projects.
